# Internal Liquidity

## Definition
Internal Liquidity refers to **stop clusters and resting orders inside the current price range** — i.e., between the current swing high and swing low. These are the intermediate targets that price sweeps before reaching [[External Liquidity]] (the major swing points beyond the range).

Think of internal liquidity as "speed bumps." Price often hits internal liquidity levels before continuing to the final draw. When it hits internal liquidity with significant displacement, it may reverse — use that as a signal to exit partial positions.

*Example:* Price is in a bullish move. The range is 17,000–17,400. Inside the range, there's a minor swing high at 17,250. That 17,250 level has [[Buy-Side Liquidity]] (stop-losses from shorts at the breakout level). It's internal liquidity. Price will likely tap it, pause or slightly retrace, then continue to 17,400 ([[External Liquidity]]).

## Rules
- Mark all minor swing highs/lows inside the current range as internal liquidity targets
- Internal liquidity is the **first take-profit target** — never the final one unless structure changes
- If price hits internal liquidity with a strong [[Displacement Candle]] and reverses, protect capital — do not hold blindly
- [[Equal Highs]] and [[Equal Lows]] within the range are high-probability internal liquidity targets
- Price often forms a [[Fair Value Gap]] above/below internal liquidity after sweeping it
- Internal liquidity is cleared first before the market moves to external liquidity — be patient
