# Buy-Side Liquidity

## Definition
Buy-Side Liquidity (BSL) refers to **resting stop-loss orders sitting above price** — specifically above swing highs, previous day highs, equal highs, and session highs. These stops belong to retail short-sellers who place their stops above resistance.

From an institutional perspective, these stops represent **buy orders at scale**. When a large institution wants to sell a massive position, it needs buyers. BSL is a pool of those buyers — so institutions engineer price upward into that zone to get their sell orders filled, then reverse price downward.

*Example:* EURUSD has an obvious resistance level at 1.0850. Retail traders short there and place stops at 1.0860. Buy-side liquidity pools at 1.0860. A smart money player wants to sell 500 lots — they push price up to 1.0862, fill against those stop-triggered buys, then drive price down.

## Rules
- Mark BSL above: swing highs, previous day highs, equal highs, session highs, and obvious double-top patterns
- A [[Liquidity Sweep]] of BSL should be followed by a bearish displacement — the sweep runs stops then reverses
- BSL is a **draw on liquidity** target for bullish moves — price reaches up to collect it before reversing
- In a bullish scenario, BSL above is the take-profit target (price is drawn toward it, not away)
- After BSL is swept, look for a [[Fair Value Gap]] or [[IFVG]] forming on the lower TF for the short entry
- Combine BSL location with [[Higher Timeframe Bias]] — never fade BSL if HTF is bullish without strong confirmation
