# External Liquidity

## Definition
External Liquidity refers to **stop clusters and resting orders beyond the current price range** — above the major swing high or below the major swing low. These are the macro targets that institutions are ultimately driving price toward. When external liquidity is swept, the move is complete.

External liquidity is where the big money is — it represents the stops of longer-term positions and the liquidity needed to fill large institutional orders.

*Example:* On a daily chart, there's a prior swing high at 4,800. Price is currently ranging between 4,600 and 4,750. The 4,800 level is external liquidity — a major BSL pool. When [[Higher Timeframe Bias]] is bullish, this is the ultimate draw.

## Rules
- External liquidity is the **final take-profit target** — the end of the move
- When external liquidity is swept, look for signs of reversal — the move may be over
- Major swing highs (for [[Buy-Side Liquidity]]) and major swing lows (for [[Sell-Side Liquidity]]) are external liquidity
- Price must clear [[Internal Liquidity]] before reaching external liquidity — don't rush to the final target
- After external liquidity is swept, a major [[Change of Character]] often follows
- The higher the timeframe of the external liquidity, the more significant the reversal after sweep
- Monthly/weekly swing highs and lows are the most powerful external liquidity targets
